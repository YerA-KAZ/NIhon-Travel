Japan Etiquette Guide

Understanding Japanese customs shows respect and opens doors to more authentic experiences. Here's what every visitor should know before arriving.

Home› Travel Tips› Etiquette

Why Etiquette Matters

🎌 Japan's Unspoken Rules

Japan is one of the world's most etiquette-conscious cultures. While Japanese people are forgiving of tourists making small mistakes, showing awareness of local customs signals respect and will be genuinely appreciated. Most rules stem from the core values of wa (harmony), consideration for others, and cleanliness.

Don't be overwhelmed — the vast majority of rules are simply about being quiet, tidy and considerate. If in doubt: watch what locals do and follow their lead.

😊 Japanese people are extremely welcoming to tourists. Even if you make a mistake, a sincere bow and "sumimasen" (sorry/excuse me) will resolve almost any situation gracefully.

General Rules

⚖️ Essential Dos & Don'ts

✅ ALWAYS DO
  • Remove your shoes when entering homes, ryokan, and many traditional restaurants
  • Bow slightly when greeting, thanking, or apologizing to someone
  • Queue patiently in single-file lines at stations, escalators, and shops
  • Keep your voice low in public spaces, especially on trains and in temples
  • Carry your trash until you find a rubbish bin — there are very few in Japan
  • Use two hands when giving or receiving business cards, gifts, or money
  • Say "itadakimasu" before eating and "gochisousama" when finished at a restaurant
❌ NEVER DO
  • Tip anyone — ever. In Japan, tipping is considered rude or even offensive
  • Eat or drink while walking (except at festivals — it's fine there)
  • Talk on your phone on trains and public transport
  • Point at people with your index finger — use your whole hand instead
  • Stick chopsticks upright in rice — it resembles funeral incense offerings
  • Pass food from chopstick to chopstick — another funeral gesture
  • Enter a bathtub or onsen without showering thoroughly first

Hot Springs

♨️ Onsen (Hot Spring) Etiquette

Onsen bathing is one of Japan's greatest pleasures, but it comes with specific rules that must be followed. Breaking them can be deeply disrespectful to other bathers.

1
Shower Before Entering the Bath
Always use the shower stations provided to wash your entire body before entering the onsen pool. This is non-negotiable — the bath is for soaking, not washing.
2
Bathe Nude — Swimwear is Not Allowed
Traditional onsen are taken in the nude, in gender-separated baths. Mixed-gender rotenburo (outdoor baths) sometimes allow towels but check signs. Swimsuits are only allowed at "swimming pool" style facilities.
3
Small Towel Stays Out of the Water
You can bring a small modesty towel into the bathing area, but do not dip it in the onsen water. Fold it on your head (a common Japanese practice) or place it on the side.
4
No Photos in the Bathing Area
Absolutely no cameras or phones. This is both a privacy and decency rule. Leave all devices in the locker room. Violation can result in removal from the facility.
5
Tattoos May Be Restricted
Many traditional onsen prohibit tattoos due to historical associations with organized crime. Tattoo-friendly onsen (and private "kashikiri" baths for exclusive hire) are increasingly available — search specifically for these.

Sacred Spaces

⛩️ Temple & Shrine Etiquette

✅ AT SHRINES (Shinto)
  • Bow once before passing through the torii gate
  • Wash hands at the temizuya (water basin) before approaching the main hall
  • Toss a coin (any denomination) into the offertory box
  • Bow twice, clap twice, pray silently, then bow once more (2-2-1 method)
  • Speak quietly and move respectfully on the grounds
✅ AT TEMPLES (Buddhist)
  • Purify hands at the water basin if available
  • Light incense (senko) and waft smoke towards yourself for good health
  • Place hands together in a prayer gesture (gassho) — do not clap
  • Remove hats before entering indoor halls
  • Photography is often allowed outside — check for indoor restrictions
💡 Shrine vs Temple: Shrines (jinja) are Shinto, marked by torii gates. Temples (tera/ji) are Buddhist, often with large gates (sanmon) and incense burners. Japan has both and most sites welcome visitors of all beliefs.

Food Culture

🍜 Dining Etiquette

🥢
Chopstick Rules
Never point with chopsticks, stab food, or wave them. Don't stick them upright in rice (funeral symbol). Pass food with hands, not chopstick-to-chopstick.
🍵
Slurping is OK
Slurping noodles and soup is normal and even considered a compliment to the chef. It also cools the food. Don't feel embarrassed — join in.
🫗
Pouring Drinks
Pour drinks for others at your table before pouring for yourself. Hold your glass when someone pours for you. "Kanpai!" (cheers) before the first sip.
💴
Paying the Bill
Pay at the counter/register, not at the table. Place cash in the small tray provided — don't hand money directly. Never tip. The price includes full service.
🧹
Clean Up After Yourself
In ramen shops and food courts, return your tray and sort your rubbish. In traditional restaurants, neatly stack dishes and replace chopstick holders.
🎁
Receiving Food as a Gift
Accept with both hands and a bow. Say "sumimasen" (thank you for your consideration). Don't open the gift immediately in front of the giver — it's considered impolite.
